Toki Pona

Developed by linguist Sonja Lang, Toki Pona is a language with around 130 words.

The shows uses Latin spellings. Pronounce these per IPA standards. The alphabet is:
a e i j k l m n o p s t u w

Basic Sentences

The particle li separates the subject from the predicate.

  • soweli li moku - The cat is eating.
  • jan li lape - People sleep.

There is no verb "to be". The part after li can also be a noun or an adjective phrase.

  • kili li moku - Fruits are food.
  • telo li pona - Water is good.

If the subject is mi or sina alone, li is always omitted.

  • mi moku - I eat.
  • sina pona - You are good.

Ambiguity

Toki Pona words are less specific that English, so direct translation is challenging.

  • mi moku - I am eating, I was eating, or I will be eating.

Many words have multiple or general meanings.

  • soweli - A cat, dog, or any land mammal.
  • kili - Any fruit or vegetable.

Most words can act as nouns, verbs, adjectives, or adverbs.

  • telo - Water, wet, or to wash.
  • pona - Good, to improve, or wellness.

Nouns have no number and there is no "a" or "the".

  • kala - A fish, the fish, some fish.
  • mi - I, me, we, or us.

Direct objects

The particle e signifies the direct object. This also makes the word before a verb.

  • soweli li moku e telo - The animal drinks the water.
  • mi telo e luka - I wash my hands.
  • sina suli e ona - You enlarge it.

Modifying Words

Words can be modified adding other words directly after them.

  • tomo mi - My house.
  • pilin pona - A good feeling.

To negate a word, put ala after it.

  • mi lape ala - I'm not sleeping.
  • jan ala li toki - No one responded.

Each additional modifier affects the very first word.

  • lipu kasi tu - Two plant lists.
  • poki laso pimeja - A blue and black box.

You can insert pi between words to change this behavior.

  • lipu pi kasi tu - A two-plant list.
  • poki pi laso pimeja - A dark-blue box.

Prepositions

The words kepeken, lon, sama, tan, and tawa can start prepositional phrases at the end of a sentence.

  • mi moku kepeken ilo - I eat with cutlery.
  • soweli li lon tomo - The rat is in the hall.
  • sina kalama sama waso - You sing like birds!
  • mi kama tan pali - I'm back from work.
  • ona li toki e ni tawa sina - They said that to you.

Questions

There are three ways to form a question. If you want a yes-or-no answer, use verb ala verb

  • ona li musi ala musi - Is she playing?

This will usually result in one of three types of replies:

  • musi - Yes: [she is] playing.
  • musi ala - No: [she is] not playing.
  • ala - No: [she is] not [playing].

If you want to check something, end the sentence with anu seme

  • sina seli anu seme - Are you making fire?
  • soweli suwi li lon poki anu seme - Is the cute cat in a box?

Replace unknown info with seme to form an open-ended question.

  • jan seme li pana e moku telo - Who's bringing the drinks?
  • sina pali e seme - What are you making?

Conjunctions

There are many ways to say "and". Use en for 2+ subjects.

  • mi en sina li musi mute - You and I are playing a lot.

Repeat li for 2+ predicates.

  • soweli ni li lili li suwi - This animal is small and cute.

Repeat e for 2+ objects.

  • ona li lukin e waso e kala - She sees the birds and fish.

Do the same for prepositonal phrases.

  • mi pali e tomo kepeken palisa kepeken kiwen - I built the hut with sticks and stones.

You can use anu to mean "or".

  • mi tawa esun anu ma kasi - We'll either go to the store or to the park.
  • pipi anu akesi li lon - That's either an insect or a lizard.

Interjections

Interjections are common. Add a for emphasis.

  • pona - Good!; Great!; Thanks!
  • toki - Hello!; Hi!; Greetings!
  • lon a - True!; Correct!

Loanwords

Toki Pona uses proper adjectives, not nouns. These are marked by a box. Only the first sound of each word is read.

  • jan [sona olin nasa jasima alasa] - A person named Sonja, a.k.a. jan Sonja.
  • toki [tan open sin ijo] - The language "Tosi" (Deutsch), a.k.a. German.
  • ma [mani esun wawa insa kule awen] li suli - The land Mewika (USA) is big.

Loanwords should try to follow Toki Pona's rules for syllables:

  • They have exactly one vowel.
  • They may have n at the end.
  • The first syllable in a word may start with a vowel.
  • The others must all start with a consonant.
  • The combinations wu, wo, ji, ti, nm and nn are avoided.

The word o

You can use o before a verb to turn it into a command.

  • o kute - Listen!
  • o moku - Please drink.

You can use o after a subject to address them.

  • jan [pona anu pana en] o - Pape!

You can place o between subject and predicate to express a wish, desire, request, etc.

  • pona o tawa sina - Joy to you.
  • mi o pali - I should be working.

Or it can also be a contraction of the first two uses.

  • soweli [tomo open nasin] o moku - Eat, Ton.

Preverbs

Preverbs go before verbs to modify their meaning.

  • mi ken sitelen - I can write.
  • waso lili li wile kama suli - The small bird wants to become big.

The words wile, kama, sona, lukin, ken, awen, and alasa can be preverbs.

Context

The word la can be used to mark the context of a sentence: a la b means: In the context of a, b. This can mean time, cause, topic, etc.

  • sina lon poka mi la mi pilin pona - When you are by my side, I feel good.

The word la is often used to move a prepositional phrase to the front of the sentence.

  • sama mi la sina sona mute - Just like me, you know a lot.
  • tan seme la sina seli - Why are you hot?

The preposition lon can be omitted in this case.

  • tenpo pimeja la mi lape - I sleep [at] night.

Numbers

Combine number words to add them up.

For exmaple, wan is 1, tu is 2, luka is 5, mute is 20, ale is 100, tu tu is 4, and luka tu wan is 8.

The word nanpa can be used to mark ordinals.

  • lipu nanpa wan li pona - The first book is good.
  • nasin nanpa mute tu wan - 23rd Street.
